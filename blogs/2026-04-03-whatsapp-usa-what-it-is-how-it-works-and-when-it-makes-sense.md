<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Can you use WhatsApp in the USA?</strong></h2>



<p>Yes. You can use WhatsApp in the U.S. just like you’d use it in most other countries. Here’s some things to know about it:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>You can register with a U.S. phone number (+1 country code)</li>



<li>You can send messages and make voice and video calls</li>



<li>You can use it over Wi-Fi or mobile data</li>



<li>Both you and the person you’re messaging need the app installed.</li>
</ul>



<p>The part that surprises most people is that WhatsApp isn’t tied to your carrier. It doesn’t use SMS or MMS. It’s an app-to-app messaging service that runs over the internet.</p>



<p>If you’re wondering if people in the U.S. use WhatsApp, they certainly do. In July 2025, Zuckerberg&nbsp;<a href="https://www.theverge.com/2024/7/25/24205421/whatsapp-meta-apple-ios-android-smartphone">posted</a>&nbsp;on his&nbsp;<a href="https://sinch.com/blog/whatsapp-channels-marketing-companies/">WhatsApp Channel</a>&nbsp;that the app passed 100 million U.S. monthly active users (MAUs). That’s up roughly two million year over year, making the U.S. one of WhatsApp’s fastest-growing markets.&nbsp;That makes learning how to use WhatsApp a smart investment.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>How does WhatsApp work in the USA?</strong></h2>



<p>WhatsApp works the same way all around the world. It sends messages over the internet instead of through your mobile carrier.</p>



<p>Here’s what that means for you:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Internet-based messaging:</strong> Messages are sent using Wi-Fi or mobile data, not SMS</li>



<li><strong>Phone number identity: </strong>Your account is tied to your phone number, even though messages aren’t sent through carrier networks</li>



<li><strong>App-to-app communication: </strong>Both users need to have WhatsApp installed</li>



<li><strong>Cross-device support: </strong>You can use WhatsApp on your phone and desktop at the same time</li>



<li><strong>Media-rich messaging: </strong>Photos, videos, documents, and voice notes are supported natively</li>
</ul>



<p>If you’re used to texting in the U.S., using WhatsApp is similar, but with a lot of added features that make for a rich experience. Some businesses even use <a href="https://sinch.com/messaging/whatsapp-business-api/">WhatsApp</a> to let their customers shop or get support directly within the app.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>How to set up WhatsApp with a U.S. phone number</strong></h2>



<p>Setting up WhatsApp in the U.S. only takes a few minutes and is similar to downloading any other app and creating an account.</p>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Download WhatsApp</strong> from the <a href="https://apps.apple.com/us/app/whatsapp-messenger/id310633997">App Store</a> or <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.whatsapp&amp;hl=en_US">Google Play</a></li>



<li><strong>Open the app</strong> and agree to the terms</li>



<li><strong>Select United States (+1)</strong> as your country</li>



<li><strong>Enter your U.S. phone number</strong></li>



<li><strong>Verify by SMS or voice call</strong></li>



<li><strong>Set up your profile </strong>(with your name and photo if you’d like)</li>
</ol>



<p>It’s as simple as that. There’s no separate “U.S. version” to install or any add-ons to download.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Does WhatsApp cost money in the USA?</strong></h2>



<p>WhatsApp doesn’t charge per message, but it does use data. Check with your carrier to see how much you’ll be charged for using data.</p>



<p>Here’s what you need to know about costs for WhatsApp:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>There are no per-message fees (unlike SMS/MMS)</li>



<li>It uses Wi-Fi or mobile data</li>



<li>International messaging is free (there are no carrier fees)</li>



<li>Roaming charges may apply if you’re using mobile data abroad</li>
</ul>



<p>Want to avoid unexpected charges? Use WhatsApp on Wi-Fi, especially if you’re traveling.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Who uses WhatsApp in the USA?</strong></h2>



<p>Per&nbsp;<a href="https://sqmagazine.co.uk/whatsapp-statistics/">SQ magazine</a>, WhatsApp holds 32% market share in North America, but usage varies widely by demographics and location.</p>



<p><a href="https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/fact-sheet/social-media/">As a recent Pew Research Center study shows</a><strong>, Hispanic Americans make up the largest group of WhatsApp users in the U.S.</strong>, with 56% being active on the messaging app, followed by Asian Americans (39%),&nbsp; African Americans (36%), and White Americans (22%).</p>



<p>There are also&nbsp;<strong>generational differences in WhatsApp usage</strong>. Interestingly, users between 18 and 29 years are about as active on WhatsApp as users between 50 and 64.</p>

<ul class="wp-block-list">

<p><strong>WhatsApp is also slightly more popular with higher earners</strong>. 37% of WhatsApp users in the U.S. make $100,000 per year or more, compared to 26% of WhatsApp users earning $30,000 per year or less.</p>



<p>When it comes to location,&nbsp;<strong>urbanites are more active on WhatsApp than rural residents</strong>, with 39% of urban residents using WhatsApp, followed by 31% of users from suburban and 19% of users from rural areas. In recent years, cities like Washington D.C. and New York City have actively taken to WhatsApp to either <a href="https://www.axios.com/local/washington-dc/2022/02/15/dc-mayor-whatsapp-records-concerns">communicate among staff</a> or <a href="https://new.mta.info/press-release/mta-new-york-city-transit-now-using-whatsapp-to-communicate-with-customers">to reach more citizens</a>.</p>



<p>Clearly, WhatsApp has a large (and growing) user base in the U.S., but it’s still not the default messaging channel for most Americans. Adoption is uneven, with most usage in international communities, among younger users, in urban areas, and if people need cross-border communication.</p>



<p>That’s what many businesses get wrong. They see “100+ million users” and assume WhatsApp has universal reach. In reality, your reach depends heavily on who your audience is.</p>

<p>The bottom line? If you need maximum reach, use SMS. If you can manage only sending messages to Apple users, then iMessage will work. RCS is improving, but isn’t universal yet. With WhatsApp, you get a powerful messaging channel, but your success could be audience dependent.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Is WhatsApp safe in the USA?</strong></h2>



<p>Yes, but there are important things to know. WhatsApp uses end-to-end encryption, which means only you and the recipient can read your messages. Not even WhatsApp (which is owned by Meta) can access message content.</p>



<p>WhatsApp protects your message content (texts, calls, and media) and conversations between users, and encryption is included by default – no setup required.</p>



<p>But there are some things it doesn’t protect. WhatsApp gets information from your metadata, like who you talk to and how often, as well as cloud backups if they’re stored outside of encrypted environments. And if your phone is compromised, WhatsApp doesn’t protect against device security risks.</p>



<p>So, while WhatsApp is secure for messaging, it’s not invisible. That distinction is important, especially for business communications and use cases where compliance is key.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Can I use WhatsApp on its own for my business?</strong></h2>



<p>WhatsApp isn’t just a way to talk to your friends and family, it can also be used for your business communications. But if you’re using it for business, you can’t use the regular app.</p>



<p>For personal use, there’s WhatsApp messenger, which isn’t designed for business purposes. If you need it for small business communication, you can use the <a href="https://sinch.com/blog/whatsapp-business-multiple-users/">WhatsApp Business app</a>, which offers simple, free, manual messaging. Once you start scaling conversation, you’ll probably want to move on to the <a href="https://sinch.com/blog/whatsapp-business-api">WhatsApp Business Platform</a>, which is API-based and offers automatic and integrations.</p>



<p>Deciding between them? The real question is scale. For 1:1 conversations, you can use the Business app. For automated, multi-agent support, you can use the platform. And most importantly: for anything customer-facing, don’t use the personal app.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>When does WhatsApp make sense for U.S. businesses?</strong></h2>



<p>Because WhatsApp isn’t the biggest way people contact each other in the U.S., it works best in specific scenarios.</p>



<p>Use WhatsApp when:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Your audience includes international customers</li>



<li>You have cross-border communication needs</li>



<li>Customers are already using WhatsApp to reach you</li>



<li>You need rich, conversational support threads</li>
</ul>



<p>Use SMS or RCS when:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>You need maximum reach across all users</li>



<li>You’re sending alerts, reminders, or notifications</li>



<li>You’re running time-sensitive campaigns</li>
</ul>



<p>Use an <a href="https://sinch.com/omnichannel/">omnichannel</a> approach when:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Your audience is mixed</li>



<li>You want fallback delivery (e.g., SMS if WhatsApp isn’t available)</li>



<li>You’re managing customer journeys across touchpoints.</li>
</ul>



<p>Most successful teams in the U.S. land where WhatsApp is <em>one</em> channel, not <em>the</em> channel.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Is WhatsApp worth it for U.S. businesses?</strong></h2>



<p>By now, you should have a solid understanding about how to use WhatsApp in the U.S. and its potential business applications.</p>



<p>Whether or not it’s worth it depends on your audience. If your customers already use WhatsApp, it can be a powerful engagement channel. If they don’t, you’ll be sending messages out into the abyss, and adoption will backfire.</p>



<p>The companies that see the best results treat WhatsApp as part of a broader messaging strategy along with SMS, email, and emerging channels like <a href="https://sinch.com/rcs/">RCS</a>, as opposed to a replacement for them.</p>



<p>As the popularity of WhatsApp is growing in the U.S., it’ll be interesting to see how businesses adapt the app in the future. </p>
