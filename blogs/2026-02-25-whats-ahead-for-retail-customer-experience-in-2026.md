<p>The retail industry has always been shaped by changing consumer behavior, but those changes are happening faster than they used to.</p>



<p>Now, shoppers move easily between online and in-store experiences, expect updates in real time, and notice quickly when something feels disconnected or unclear. Whether they’re tracking a delivery or deciding whether a promotion is worth their attention, communication determines how the experience is perceived.</p>



<p>Late last year, we released <a href="https://sinch.com/blog/customer-communications-predictions/">Sinch Predictions 2026</a>, a research-based look at how customer communications are expected to evolve in the year ahead. <strong>The themes were consistent across industries: more AI-driven conversations, higher expectations for trust and relevance, and growing pressure for brands to meet customers on their terms.</strong></p>



<p>For retailers, these shifts feel especially immediate because communications touch every stage of the shopping journey.</p>



<p>So, what big shifts will shape retail customer communications in 2026?</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">1. AI agents will spark an explosion in retail conversation volume and opportunity</h2>



<p>Retail messaging has long been built around updates and promotions like order confirmations, shipping notifications, and sale alerts. These notifications are essential, but they have rarely been designed to invite interaction.</p>



<p>That&#8217;s starting to change.</p>



<p>Shoppers increasingly expect to be able to ask questions and resolve issues in the moment. When a delivery is delayed or an item is out of stock, they want answers immediately, not a link to a help center or a long wait for support.</p>


    <div class="callout text-body-color px-5 py-6 px-md-6 px-lg-7 py-md-7 longform-spacings rounded-lg bg-primary">

        <div class="content-body"> <p class="mb-0">Sinch’s <a href="https://sinch.com/state-of-customer-communications/chapter/explore-customer-communication-insights-from-your-industry?utm_content=blog&amp;utm_term=&amp;intcmp=GL-25-Brn_SCC_blog_content">State of retail communications</a> research found that <b>around 28% of consumers say they get frustrated when they can’t ask a question in response to a transactional message</b>. With AI agents, it becomes increasingly possible to turn these routine updates into real conversations.</p></div>
    </div>



<p>For example, imagine a retailer’s delivery notification having options to reschedule or ask a question to an AI agent without leaving the message thread. A back-in-stock alert can become a conversation that confirms size availability or suggests similar items. Each interaction increases conversation volume, but it also creates opportunities to resolve issues faster and keep shoppers moving forward.</p>



<p>As AI agents become more capable of initiating conversations on their own, proactive outreach will also become more common. An AI agent can notice when a shopper repeatedly checks an order status and reach out with an update before frustration builds. Over time, these small moments add up, and retailers should expect conversation volume to grow as more messages are designed for genuine engagement rather than one-way updates.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">2. AI agents will evolve from simple cost savers to true growth engines for retail brands</h2>



<p>Retail automation has long been about efficiency, where chatbots and automated responses were designed to reduce support volume and close interactions quickly. That still matters, but it’s no longer the whole picture.</p>



<p>As AI agents become better at understanding context and intent, they’re starting to support growth across the customer journey – and shoppers are open to this. In fact, <strong>more than </strong><a href="https://sinch.com/state-of-customer-communications/chapter/explore-customer-communication-insights-from-your-industry?utm_content=blog&amp;utm_term=&amp;intcmp=GL-25-Brn_SCC_blog_content"><strong>70% of consumers</strong></a><strong> say they’re willing to interact with an AI shopping assistant built for retail</strong>. This makes space for conversations that go beyond resolving issues one at a time.</p>



<figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Chart showing that more than 70% of consumers say personalized product recommendations from a retail brand’s AI chatbot would be helpful. " class="wp-image-174020" height="567" src="https://sinch.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/SDCC-25-Chapter-2-Chart-7_v2-EN-1024x567.png" width="1024" /><figcaption class="wp-element-caption"><em>Shoppers are open to AI when it adds value. More than 70% say interacting with an AI shopping assistant would be helpful. Source: Sinch’s State of retail communications, 2025.</em></figcaption></figure>



<p>Consider a customer who completes a purchase and receives a confirmation message. An AI agent can follow up with delivery options, product care tips, or a relevant accessory recommendation while the interaction is still active. After a return, an AI agent can suggest alternatives that fit the shopper’s preferences, keeping the relationship moving forward.</p>



<p>We expect AI agents will also play a larger role in retention. When browsing behavior drops or loyalty activity slows, an AI can check in with support or a timely incentive. For shoppers, this feels helpful. For retailers, it turns service interactions into moments with long-term value.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">3. Voice AI will become the preferred channel for complex conversations</h2>



<p>When an issue feels urgent or complicated, many shoppers would rather talk than type. That might include situations like problems with a high-value order, delivery issues during peak shopping periods, or situations where they need clarity and reassurance.</p>



<p>Traditional automated voice systems have struggled to meet those expectations, and have trained customers to associate voice automation with frustration. Voice AI is changing that, which could be why around <a href="https://sinch.com/blog/retail-communication-takeaways/"><strong>65% of retail leaders said their businesses plan to adopt AI voice assistants</strong></a><strong> in the next year.</strong></p>



<p>Sinch’s Director of Programmable Voice, Sofia Schönbeck, says advances in speech-to-speech technology will soon allow voice AI agents to respond quickly and understand intent in real time. For shoppers, this could mean they could explain an issue in their own words and get relevant help without navigating rigid menus. For retailers, this means complex conversations can be handled more efficiently while still feeling human.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">4. Conversational messaging will redefine retail customer expectations</h2>



<p>Consumer expectations around messaging have changed. People are used to easy, back-and-forth conversations in their personal lives, and they increasingly expect the same from the brands they shop with.</p>



<p>In retail, one-way messages are quickly becoming a source of friction. Around 28% of consumers say they get frustrated when they can’t ask questions or get support from informational message. When an update arrives that they can’t reply to, it creates extra effort and often delays resolution. As a result, conversational messaging is starting to feel like a basic expectation rather than a differentiator.</p>



<p>This shift is most visible during peak retail moments like Black Friday and Cyber Monday, when customers are tracking orders, watching delivery timelines, and looking for quick answers. In 2025, this behavior directly shaped how retailers communicated. The use of <a href="https://sinch.com/blog/what-is-rcs-messaging/">RCS messaging</a> had the strongest year-over-year gains across the Sinch platform, climbing <a href="https://sinch.com/blog/bfcm-breakdown/">144% YoY</a> as more brands used rich, verified messages to handle real-time updates and customer questions at scale.</p>

Retailers that continue to rely on broadcast-style messaging will find it harder to meet expectations as conversational experiences become the norm, especially during high-pressure moments when clarity and speed matter most.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">5. Connected customer journeys will unlock brand loyalty and long-term value</h2>



<p>Retail journeys often span multiple channels. A shopper might discover a product through email, ask a question via chat, and receive delivery updates by text. When those interactions don’t connect, the experience feels fragmented and frustrating.</p>



<p>Most retailers are working toward integration, but there’s still uneven maturity. Today, <a href="https://sinch.com/state-of-customer-communications/chapter/explore-customer-communication-insights-from-your-industry?utm_content=blog&amp;utm_term=&amp;intcmp=GL-25-Brn_SCC_blog_content"><strong>59% of retailers</strong></a><strong> say their customer communications are fully integrated with their tech stacks</strong>, while <strong>31% say they’re only somewhat integrated</strong>. For consumers, that shows up as broken handoffs and repeated questions.</p>

<p>AI will help make this possible by summarizing interactions and passing context seamlessly between systems and agents. A service representative will see the full history before responding, and an AI agent will understand what has already happened in the journey.</p>



<p>For retailers, this connection across channels reduces friction and creates experiences that feel intentional rather than pieced together.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">6. Regionalized communication strategies will define the true leaders in retail</h2>



<p>Retail may be a global business, but customer communication is not. Channel usage, timing expectations, and local norms may vary widely from one region to the next.</p>



<p>This year, successful global retailers will need to move beyond one-size-fits-all messaging strategies. They&#8217;ll balance global consistency with local expertise, making sure messages feel relevant in each market while still aligning with brand standards.</p>



<p>The retail industry is already ahead of many others when it comes to adopting channels like WhatsApp, Messenger, and Instagram, with <a href="https://sinch.com/state-of-customer-communications/chapter/explore-customer-communication-insights-from-your-industry?utm_content=blog&amp;utm_term=&amp;intcmp=GL-25-Brn_SCC_blog_content"><strong>56% of retailers</strong></a><strong> saying they communicate with consumers on these platforms</strong>. That makes retail strategy even more important.</p>



<p>Success will depend on understanding which channels dominate in specific regions, how shoppers prefer to engage at different stages of the journey, and how local regulations shape communication. Retailers that invest in regional insight will be better positioned to deliver experiences that resonate rather than relying on assumptions that don&#8217;t translate across markets.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">7. Verified and secure communications will define the future of trust for retail brands</h2>



<p>Retail communications often involve a consumer’s personal and financial data, from payment confirmations to account updates and delivery notifications. At the same time, consumers are more cautious than ever due to the rise of scams and spoofed messages.</p>



<p>In 2026, trust will need to be visible from the moment a message arrives. Verified sender identities, recognizable branding, and clear indicators of legitimacy will become critical for engagement. Customers should be able to tell at a glance that a message is real and safe to interact with.</p>



<p>But many retailers still have room to improve here. <strong>In 2025, just 26% of retailers said they were currently using RCS in 2025, compared to 37.5% across all industries.</strong> This is a missed opportunity when channels like RCS support branded, <a href="https://sinch.com/blog/rcs-verified-senders/">verified sender profiles</a> that help establish trust before a customer ever opens a message.</p>

Retailers that make trust obvious and effortless will stand out as communication volumes continue to grow.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">8. Intelligent inboxes will reinvent email as a precision channel</h2>



<p><a href="https://www.mailgun.com/resources/guides/email-supply-chain-communication/">Email remains a core part of retail communications</a>, and nearly half of consumers say it&#8217;s the best channel to receive messages from retailers. At the same time, inboxes are becoming more selective. AI-powered filtering and summarization are already shaping which messages get attention and which are ignored.</p>



<p>This year, <a href="https://www.mailgun.com/blog/deliverability/inbox-optimization-why-the-future-of-email-belongs-to-humans-and-machines/">intelligent inboxes will reward relevance over volume</a>. Generic promotions and loosely targeted campaigns will struggle to reach primary inboxes. Messages that clearly reflect customer intent will perform better. This is a shift many marketers are already feeling, as Vandita Arora, Group Product Manager at HubSpot, explained in our <a href="https://sinch.com/webinars/sinch-predictions-2026/">Sinch Predictions 2026 webinar</a>.</p>

<p>The retailers who focus on these moves in 2026 will be better prepared for what’s ahead because they chose to make communication clearer, more relevant, and more human. That is where preparation turns into advantage.</p>
